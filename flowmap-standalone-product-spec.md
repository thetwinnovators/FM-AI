# FlowMap Standalone Product Specification

## Overview

FlowMap should be repositioned from an internal Flowerk intelligence console into a standalone topic discovery, research, and learning product focused on AI, technology, automation, Claude, Codex, vibecoding, and adjacent emerging concepts.[cite:40][cite:38][cite:4][cite:5] The current Flow Map design already provides a strong foundation for this shift because it includes a pipeline strip, a graph-based exploration surface, a glass sidebar, KPI cards, and a seed memory panel that can evolve into a persistent topic knowledge base.[cite:57]

The product’s core job is to continuously find relevant videos, articles, and public social posts, organize them into structured topic pages, reveal relationships across concepts and tools, and help the member build practical skills through an education layer.[cite:57][cite:40] The system should remain rule-based wherever possible, while using AI selectively for parsing, classification, intent understanding, summarization, and related-topic inference when deterministic logic becomes brittle.[cite:40]

## Product vision

FlowMap is a topic intelligence workspace that maps the AI and technology landscape around the member’s interests, continuously finds new learning material, and turns scattered media into a structured knowledge system.[cite:40] Instead of behaving like a generic scraper, it should behave like a research assistant with memory, graph navigation, explainable discovery logic, and an education engine tailored to the member’s goals.[cite:38][cite:40]

### Product promise

- Discover new content across videos, articles, and public social posts.[cite:40]
- Organize discoveries into topic pages, categories, tags, and graph relationships.[cite:57][cite:40]
- Learn what the member follows and suggest adjacent topics or creators to track.[cite:38][cite:40]
- Turn repeated concepts into structured learning content for AI and vibecoding growth.[cite:38]

## Why this product should exist

The value is not raw scraping. The value is structured discovery, persistent topic memory, cross-media organization, and skill acceleration for fast-moving subjects like AI tooling, agent frameworks, Claude workflows, Codex usage, automation patterns, and vibecoding techniques.[cite:38][cite:40] The existing Flow Map UI already tells a story of inputs, memory, signals, and outcomes, and that same design language can be reinterpreted as a map of how topics, content, tools, creators, and trends connect in the outside world.[cite:57]

This makes the product more durable than a simple feed reader because it combines research, organization, memory, and learning into one system.[cite:40] It also fits the user’s broader product direction toward explainable, permission-aware, pattern-recognizing systems rather than black-box recommendation engines.[cite:4][cite:5][cite:40]

## Core user outcomes

The primary member should be able to:

- Search and follow topics such as Claude, Codex, MCP, agents, prompt engineering, browser automation, RAG, design systems, and vibecoding.[cite:38][cite:40]
- See newly discovered videos, articles, and social posts related to those interests.[cite:40]
- Understand what each topic is, why it matters, and how it connects to other tools and ideas.[cite:57][cite:40]
- Save, tag, and organize content into collections or research streams.[cite:40]
- Use the Education section to learn new concepts and immediately apply them to coding, AI workflows, and automation design.[cite:38]

## Product pillars

### 1. Topic discovery

FlowMap should continuously search approved sources for content related to tracked topics and user-entered searches.[cite:40] The system should support both one-off research and persistent topic monitoring.[cite:40]

### 2. Knowledge organization

Every discovered item should be normalized into a structured system of categories, tags, entities, concepts, and relationships rather than remaining a flat feed.[cite:57][cite:40]

### 3. Visual mapping

The graph is not decoration. It is the explanation surface that shows how topics, creators, tools, companies, and content relate, much like the current Flow Map uses shared selection state and node relationships to explain internal intelligence behavior.[cite:57]

### 4. Persistent memory

FlowMap should learn what the member cares about, which sources they trust, which content types they save most often, and which adjacent topics repeatedly attract their attention.[cite:38][cite:40] This memory should stay reviewable, permission-aware, and explainable.[cite:40]

### 5. Education and skill growth

The Education layer should transform repeated patterns and concepts into structured learning artifacts such as concept cards, learning paths, tool explainers, workflow examples, and adjacent-topic recommendations.[cite:38]

## Target users

### Primary user

A technical or semi-technical builder who wants to stay current on AI tools, emerging automation patterns, Claude and Codex workflows, and modern software-building techniques.[cite:38] This user values speed, context, structure, and practical applicability over passive content consumption.[cite:38][cite:40]

### Secondary users

- Founders and product builders tracking competitive or technical shifts.[cite:40]
- Developers researching toolchains, agent frameworks, and implementation examples.[cite:38]
- Designers and strategists learning AI-native workflows and vibecoding practices.[cite:38]

## Product positioning

FlowMap should be positioned as a **research map for emerging technology knowledge**, not as a general social media dashboard and not as a back-office analytics panel.[cite:57][cite:40] The graph-centered design gives it a distinctive identity, but the daily utility comes from search, topic pages, content organization, and education.[cite:57][cite:40]

A concise positioning statement is:

> FlowMap helps members discover, map, organize, and learn from the fast-moving world of AI, automation, and technical tools through a graph-powered research workspace.[cite:40]

## Information architecture

The standalone product should include six core sections.

| Section | Purpose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Home / Search | Start searches, manage tracked topics, review signals | Entry point for discovery.[cite:40] |
| Topic Pages | Structured pages for each topic, concept, or tool | Core content destination.[cite:40] |
| Discover Feed | Freshly found videos, articles, and social posts | Time-based intake layer.[cite:40] |
| Flow Map | Interactive graph of topics, creators, tools, companies, and content | Explainability and exploration surface.[cite:57][cite:40] |
| Education | Concept cards, learning paths, applied explainers, and examples | Skill-building layer.[cite:38] |
| Memory | Saved interests, learned patterns, source preferences, and suggestion controls | Persistent user intelligence layer.[cite:38][cite:40] |

## Core product flows

### 1. Start a topic

The member enters a topic such as “Claude agents,” “Codex workflows,” “browser automation,” or “vibecoding,” selects source preferences, and optionally enables ongoing monitoring.[cite:38][cite:40] FlowMap creates a topic page, seeds the graph, and schedules source discovery jobs.[cite:40]

### 2. Discover and normalize

The system fetches candidate content from configured sources, extracts metadata, deduplicates similar items, and classifies each item into topic, category, tag, and relationship models.[cite:40] Each item becomes part of the searchable knowledge layer rather than remaining raw source output.[cite:40]

### 3. Explore and save

The member browses the Discover feed, drills into a Topic page, or opens the Flow Map to understand connected entities and trends.[cite:57][cite:40] Saved items contribute to Memory and influence future topic suggestions and source prioritization.[cite:38][cite:40]

### 4. Learn and apply

The Education section transforms repeated concepts and emerging patterns into explainers, examples, and next-step learning paths so the member can upgrade AI, coding, and automation skills in a practical sequence.[cite:38]

## Screens and UX model

## Home / Search

The Home view should combine a search-first interface with a compact strategic overview of tracked topics, fresh discoveries, and learning opportunities.[cite:40] Unlike the current internal Flow Map page, which starts with operational intelligence, the standalone app should start with user intent: “What do you want to explore or keep up with?”[cite:57]

Suggested components:

- Global research search bar.
- Saved topic watchlist.
- New signals panel.
- Recently surfaced videos, articles, and posts.
- Suggested new topics based on memory.[cite:38][cite:40]

## Topic pages

Each topic page should be the canonical destination for a single topic, tool, concept, company, or creator.[cite:40] A page for “Claude,” “MCP,” or “vibecoding” should unify media, definitions, related concepts, and trend signals instead of making the member search the same thing repeatedly.[cite:38][cite:40]

Suggested sections per topic page:

- Topic summary.
- Why it matters.
- Related topics and tools.
- Latest videos.
- Latest articles.
- Latest social posts.
- Top creators and companies.
- Learning cards.
- Graph snippet.
- Save / follow controls.[cite:40]

## Discover feed

The Discover feed should act as the newest-content intake surface across all followed topics.[cite:40] Filters should support topic, source, content type, recency, signal strength, and saved state.[cite:40]

The feed should support content cards with:

- Title and source.
- Media type badge.
- Topic labels.
- Key entities.
- Relevance reason.
- Save / tag / dismiss actions.[cite:40]

## Flow Map graph

The graph remains a signature surface, but the underlying nodes should be redefined from Flowerk’s operational node taxonomy into a research-oriented taxonomy.[cite:57] The current design pattern of searchable sidebar, selection-based detail view, and cross-surface deep-linking should be preserved because it already supports explainable exploration well.[cite:57]

### Recommended node taxonomy

| Node type | Description |
|---|---|
| Topic | Core subject area such as Claude, Codex, agents, MCP, vibecoding |
| Concept | Idea or technique such as RAG, tool use, evals, prompt chaining |
| Tool | Product or framework such as Claude Code, Cursor, Codex CLI |
| Company | Anthropic, OpenAI, Vercel, etc. |
| Creator | Individuals, publications, or channels producing content |
| Video | YouTube or short-form video item |
| Article | Blog post, news article, doc page, essay |
| Social Post | Public social post or thread |
| Tag | Classification label for filtering and grouping |
| Learning Path | Structured educational sequence |
| Memory | Learned user interest or saved pattern |
| Signal | Derived trend or importance metric |

The visual style can preserve the current dark canvas, motion behavior, and sidebar treatment while changing labels, colors, and semantic meaning to reflect topic intelligence instead of workflow intelligence.[cite:57]

## Education

The Education section is a primary differentiator, not a secondary tab.[cite:38] It should help members understand new concepts and apply them to their own work in AI, software building, and automation design.[cite:38]

### Education content types

- Concept cards.
- Tool explainers.
- Workflow pattern briefs.
- Skill paths.
- “Why this matters” summaries.
- Applied examples.
- Topic primers for beginners entering an advanced area.[cite:38]

### Education structure

Each learning unit should include:

- Concept name.
- Plain-language explanation.
- Why it matters now.
- Related tools or companies.
- Example implementation or use case.
- Connected videos, articles, and posts.
- Recommended next topics.[cite:38][cite:40]

This section should become the bridge between research and action, turning discovery into upgraded capability.[cite:38]

## Memory

The Memory section should evolve from the current “Seed Memory” concept into a full user-interest knowledge base.[cite:57][cite:38][cite:40] It should store what the member follows, saves, prefers, and repeatedly revisits, and use those patterns to make new search, follow, and learning suggestions.[cite:38][cite:40]

### Memory categories

- Topics followed.
- Preferred sources.
- Frequently saved creators.
- Favorite content formats.
- Repeated technical themes.
- Adjacent-topic suggestions.
- Dismissed areas of low interest.[cite:38][cite:40]

### Memory behavior

- Member actions create evidence.
- Repeated evidence creates patterns.
- Patterns generate suggestions.
- Approved suggestions become active memory.[cite:40]

This mirrors the trusted-memory architecture already being considered for Flowerk and gives FlowMap a durable, explainable personalization layer rather than a black-box recommendation system.[cite:40]

## Rules engine

The platform should be deterministic by default.[cite:40] Rules should control discovery scope, prioritization, deduplication, tagging, scheduling, topic expansion, and content-routing behavior before AI is invoked.[cite:40]

### Rule categories

- Search rules: which keywords and variations to search for a topic.[cite:40]
- Source rules: which platforms matter for which topic types.[cite:40]
- Freshness rules: how often to search and how long content stays prioritized.[cite:40]
- Deduplication rules: how to cluster identical or near-identical content.[cite:40]
- Taxonomy rules: how to assign tags, categories, and topic relationships.[cite:40]
- Learning rules: when a repeated concept should become an Education card.[cite:38]
- Memory rules: when repeated user behavior should generate a follow suggestion.[cite:38][cite:40]

### Example rule behavior

- If topic contains `Claude`, also search `Anthropic`, `Claude Code`, `Claude API`, and `Claude workflows`.[cite:40]
- If the member repeatedly saves content about MCP and agents, suggest following tool use, orchestration, and protocol design.[cite:38][cite:40]
- If a content item has no transcript, weak metadata, and little textual context, score it lower for education extraction.[cite:40]

## AI reasoning role

AI should act as a selective reasoning layer rather than the primary control system.[cite:40] Its job is to help where deterministic rules become brittle or too expensive to maintain manually.[cite:40]

### Strong AI use cases

- Intent parsing for natural-language topic searches.[cite:40]
- Classification when rule confidence is low.[cite:40]
- Entity extraction from noisy content.[cite:40]
- Topic clustering and concept linking.[cite:40]
- Summarizing why a piece of content matters.[cite:38][cite:40]
- Generating concept explainers for Education.[cite:38]

### Areas to keep rule-based

- Crawl scope.
- Source allowlists.
- Job scheduling.
- Auto-follow behavior.
- Retention logic.
- Permission and policy controls.[cite:40]

This split preserves explainability and keeps the product aligned with a trust-led, inspectable system design.[cite:40]

## Source ingestion strategy

FlowMap should support multiple source classes while keeping each source connector independent and governable.[cite:40]

### Source classes

- Video platforms.
- Article and blog websites.
- Public documentation and newsletters.
- Public social posts and threads.[cite:40]

### Ingestion pipeline

1. Search or discover candidate URLs for a tracked topic.[cite:40]
2. Fetch page or media metadata.[cite:40]
3. Extract title, author, platform, date, text, transcript, and links when available.[cite:40]
4. Normalize into a common content schema.[cite:40]
5. Deduplicate against existing items.[cite:40]
6. Classify into topics, tags, and entities.[cite:40]
7. Update graph relationships and learning candidates.[cite:40]

## Content schema

The system should store normalized content objects rather than source-specific raw payloads.[cite:40]

### Core content entities

| Entity | Purpose |
|---|---|
| `topics` | Canonical tracked subjects |
| `topic_queries` | Search rules and keyword expansions |
| `content_items` | Unified records for videos, articles, and posts |
| `content_sources` | Source metadata and connector definitions |
| `content_entities` | Extracted companies, creators, tools, concepts |
| `tags` | Shared classification labels |
| `collections` | User-curated saved groups |
| `saved_items` | User save/bookmark state |
| `learning_cards` | Education units derived from repeated concepts |
| `watch_rules` | Persistent topic monitoring rules |
| `user_interest_patterns` | Behavioral pattern store |
| `graph_nodes` | View model for graph rendering |
| `graph_edges` | View model for graph relationships |

## Graph and memory relationship

The graph should be a rich navigation and explanation layer, but not the only source of truth.[cite:57][cite:40] Canonical data should live in normalized content, topic, and memory tables, while graph nodes and edges are generated or refreshed as a read model.[cite:57][cite:40]

This is consistent with the current Flow Map design, where the UI is powerful but the graph is not yet durably stored and is largely derived from operational data structures.[cite:57] For the standalone product, the same lesson applies: content and memory come first, graph projection second.[cite:57][cite:40]

## Design direction

The design should preserve the attached concept’s dark, technical, high-trust feel while shifting it from internal operations intelligence to external topic intelligence.[cite:57] The visual language should remain premium, cinematic, and information-dense.[cite:57]

### Recommended visual principles

- Near-black or graphite base canvas.[cite:57]
- Glassmorphic side panels and overlays.[cite:57]
- Fine neon-accent node colors by content or topic type.[cite:57]
- Sharper geometry and restrained radius.[cite:57]
- Strong content readability for detail surfaces.[cite:57]
- Subtle motion and active-state glow, not noisy animation.[cite:57]

### UI translation of current concept

| Current Flow Map element | Standalone FlowMap equivalent |
|---|---|
| Agent Intelligence Pipeline | Discovery / Parsing / Classification / Retention pipeline |
| Business entities, inputs, integrations, AI, rules | Topics, content, creators, tools, tags, memory, signals |
| Seed Memory | Interest Memory / Research Seeds |
| Derived signals | Trend signals / rising themes |
| Connected integrations | Connected sources / connectors |
| KPI cards | New discoveries, saved items, active topics, learning cards, monitored sources |

## Search and topic expansion

Search should support both exact topics and intent-driven queries such as “what are people saying about Claude Code workflows this week?” or “show me new MCP videos with implementation examples.”[cite:40] Deterministic topic-expansion rules should handle most related searches, while AI can interpret messy or ambiguous phrasing.[cite:40]

### Search capabilities

- Exact topic lookup.
- Synonym and alias matching.
- Related concept expansion.
- Source filtering.
- Time filtering.
- Content type filtering.
- Saved-interest prioritization.[cite:38][cite:40]

### Suggested expansion logic

If the member follows `Claude`, the system may also suggest:

- Anthropic.
- Claude Code.
- Claude API.
- tool use.
- MCP.
- agent workflows.
- evals.
- reasoning models.[cite:40]

## Suggested v1 scope

A focused v1 should include:

- Search-first home screen.[cite:40]
- Topic pages for followed interests.[cite:40]
- Discover feed with videos, articles, and social posts.[cite:40]
- Graph exploration view.[cite:57][cite:40]
- Education section with concept cards and explainers.[cite:38]
- Memory-backed topic suggestions.[cite:38][cite:40]
- Saved collections and watchlists.[cite:40]

This is enough to create a compelling standalone product without overextending into every possible source, automation, or recommendation behavior on day one.[cite:40]

## Future extensions

After v1, the product can expand into:

- Team research workspaces.[cite:37]
- Shared collections and collaborative tagging.[cite:37]
- Exports to docs, Notion, or knowledge bases.[cite:40]
- Daily or weekly intelligence digests.[cite:40]
- Concept-to-project suggestion workflows.[cite:38]
- More advanced memory and trust settings.[cite:40]

## Implementation recommendation

The best implementation strategy is to treat FlowMap as a standalone research product with four coordinated engines: ingestion, normalization, graph projection, and member memory.[cite:40] Rules should govern crawl scope, taxonomy, and personalization defaults, while AI should be used selectively for reasoning-heavy parsing, classification, summarization, and concept generation.[cite:40]

This approach preserves the attached design concept’s strongest qualities, aligns with the broader trusted-memory direction already being explored, and creates a differentiated product that is more useful than a simple scraper or link collector.[cite:57][cite:40][cite:38]
